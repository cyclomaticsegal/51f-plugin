# Context Recipe

Detailed guidance for the accompanying context, plus a worked example. The context is capped at 300 words. Aim for 260 to 295 so there is no risk of overrun.

## The eight moves, in detail

1. **Define the key term precisely.** The biggest failure mode is leaving the central term loose. Pin it down in one sentence, including the threshold that separates the states you care about.

2. **Decompose the concept.** Split the thing being measured into its parts so the question is not answering several questions at once. Name the parts and say which part the question is really about.

3. **Exclude.** State explicitly what does not count, and why counting it would mask the signal. Exclusions do more work than inclusions, because they stop the question from being satisfied by the wrong evidence.

4. **Trend, not snapshot, and time-box.** A snapshot tells you little, because systems present as functional while they hollow. Say that degradation or change over time is the signal, and give a start point.

5. **Name the mechanisms worth testing.** State the one or two causal pathways the question is really probing. Keep them concrete enough that someone could observe them.

6. **Give vivid markers, flagged as markers.** One or two specific, recognisable events make the question legible. Always label them as markers, not as the whole case, so the question is not reduced to a single anecdote.

7. **Set scope and weight.** Say where the binding constraint sits and weight the question toward it (a level, a region, a cohort). Generic scope produces a generic answer.

8. **Name the observable indicators.** List the concrete, measurable signals that would confirm or falsify the claim. This is the highest-leverage move. The signals you name are the spine of the question; name them specifically and say which direction confirms and which falsifies.

## Pre-submit checklist

- The statement is one falsifiable question with a named mechanism.
- Two to five mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive outcomes are proposed.
- The context defines, decomposes, excludes, time-boxes, names mechanisms, gives flagged markers, sets scope, and names observable indicators.
- The context is 300 words or fewer (count it).
- Present-day facts were verified by search, not assumed.
- No em dashes. No inevitability language.

## Worked example (generic, illustrative)

This is a neutral example to show the shape of a good triplet. It is not drawn from any real model.

Hypothesis Statement:

> To what extent are large enterprises sustaining structural hybrid remote work rather than reverting to full-time office attendance, driven by talent retention and real-estate cost pressure rather than executive preference, and is that hybrid settlement entrenching, eroding toward office mandates, or fragmenting by function?

Outcome categories:

1. Hybrid entrenched: hybrid becomes the durable default across most knowledge roles.
2. Office reversion: mandates push attendance back toward pre-2020 norms.
3. Functional fragmentation: hybrid persists for some roles and collapses for others.

Context (271 words):

> Define "structural hybrid" as a standing policy of two or more remote days per week for knowledge roles, set at the organisation level, not ad hoc manager discretion. Judge revealed policy by attendance data and lease decisions, not by stated culture.
>
> Decompose the workforce into three cohorts: roles that can be done remotely with no output loss, roles that are partly co-location dependent, and roles that are fully site bound. The question is about the first two; exclude the third, and exclude executive commentary, which routinely contradicts what firms actually enforce.
>
> Treat this as a trend from roughly 2020 to the present, not a single quarter; policies present as settled while quietly shifting. The mechanisms worth testing are talent retention (attrition risk when mandates tighten) and real-estate cost (lease consolidation locking in lower footprints), against the countervailing pull of executive preference for presence.
>
> Widely reported mandate reversals at a few large employers, and falling office lease renewals, are vivid markers of the tension, not the whole case. Weight the question toward large enterprises and knowledge roles, where the cost and retention stakes are highest, rather than small firms or site-bound work.
>
> Fix the observable indicators: average required in-office days, office lease renewal and consolidation rates, voluntary attrition tied to mandate changes, job-posting language (remote, hybrid, on-site), and per-seat real-estate spend. Hybrid holding or expanding while leases shrink and attrition tracks mandates confirms entrenchment; rising required days with stable leases signals reversion; divergence across functions signals fragmentation.

Note how the final paragraph names the specific signals. That paragraph carries the most weight: name the measurable indicators concretely and say what confirms and what falsifies.
